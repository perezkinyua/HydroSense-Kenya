## Section 1 — Level 1: Problem Framing and Python Foundation

### Scientific Context

HydroSense-Kenya addresses a practical water-management problem facing smallholder farms in Kenya: how to use existing weather and soil-sensor data to make efficient, evidence-based irrigation decisions. Poor irrigation scheduling leads either to crop water stress (under-irrigation) or wasted water and energy (over-irrigation). Both outcomes are economically damaging and environmentally costly.

### Central Scientific Question

> Given daily weather records and soil-moisture sensor readings, how can we model water availability, estimate water deficit, simulate future soil moisture, and recommend an efficient irrigation plan that minimises water use without exposing crops to moisture stress?

### Core Water Balance Model

The project is built on the simplified discrete water-balance equation:

$$S_{t+1} = S_t + R_t + I_t - ET_t - D_t$$

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| $S_t$ | Soil moisture at time $t$ (%) |
| $R_t$ | Rainfall at time $t$ (mm) |
| $I_t$ | Irrigation applied at time $t$ (mm) |
| $ET_t$ | Evapotranspiration — crop water loss (mm) |
| $D_t$ | Drainage beyond field capacity (mm) |

### Simplified ET Formula

For teaching purposes, daily evapotranspiration is estimated as:

$$ET = \max(0,\ 0.12T + 0.35W + 2.4 \cdot \text{Solar} - 0.025H)$$

where $T$ = temperature (°C), $W$ = wind speed (m/s), Solar = solar index (0–1), $H$ = humidity (%). The $\max(0, \cdot)$ ensures ET is never negative — a physical requirement.

### Assumptions and Limitations

- ET formula is empirical and simplified; it does not account for crop growth stage, soil type, or vapour pressure deficit.
- Rainfall is assumed to be fully absorbed (no surface runoff modelled).
- Sensor faults were imputed using zone medians rather than corrected at source.
- The dataset covers only 30 days (March 2026), which may not represent seasonal variability.
"""))

### 3.1 — Root Finding: Computing Required Irrigation

The irrigation problem can be framed as a root-finding problem: given current soil moisture $S_t$, today's rainfall $R_t$, and ET $ET_t$, find the irrigation amount $I$ such that:

$$S_t + R_t + I - ET_t - D_t = S_{\text{target}}$$

Rearranging: $f(I) = S_t + R_t + I - ET_t - D_t - S_{\text{target}} = 0$

This is a linear equation in $I$, so all three methods converge immediately — making it a clean test case with a known exact solution.
"""))

cells.append(md("""### 5.2 — ODE Simulation: Euler vs RK4

The soil moisture dynamics are modelled as an ordinary differential equation:

$$\frac{dS}{dt} = R(t) - ET(t) - D(t)$$

where $D(t) = \alpha \cdot \max(0, S - S_{\text{cap}})$ is drainage above field capacity and $\alpha$ is the zone drainage coefficient.

**Euler method** (first-order) takes a single linear step per day — simple but accumulates truncation error over time.

**RK4** (fourth-order Runge-Kutta) uses four intermediate slope estimates, giving much higher accuracy for the same step size. The plot below compares both methods on the Zone A data.
"""))

cells.append(code("""DRAIN_COEF = zone_a['drainage_coefficient'].iloc[0]  # 0.18 for Zone A

def soil_ode(t, S):
    \"\"\"dS/dt = R - ET - D, where D drains excess above field capacity.\"\"\"
    day = min(int(t), len(rain_arr) - 1)
    D = DRAIN_COEF * max(0.0, S - S_cap)
    return rain_arr[day] - et_arr[day] - D

S_euler = euler_integrate(soil_ode, S0=S0, t_array=t_days)
